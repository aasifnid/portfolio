Product Design · Cuemath · March 2025

Learning System Architecture

Unifying 15+ sheet types into a three-level architecture — so tutors can compose custom learning paths across grades and curricula.

LEAP locked students into rigid curriculum programs — tutors couldn't mix chapters across grades. Homework was buried, progress fragmented. I designed a three-level architecture (Chapter → Block → Node) that lets tutors compose custom paths, surfaced homework on the homepage with 7-day tracking, and centralized progress.

My Role Product Designer — IA, interaction, UI system direction
The Team 1 PM, 3 engineers, illustrator, motion designer, curriculum
Timeline 4 weeks — March 2025
Outcome Shipped to 28–30k students · 3 goal types adopted · custom paths replace locked programs

01 Context

Tutors had to dig into lessons one-by-one to assign homework. Students had no central place to see it — homework lived inside chapters, buried two layers deep. If a tutor assigned extra practice or advanced worksheets, they got lost at the bottom of the chapter page; students scrolled past them or didn't know when they were due. Tracking whether a student had started or completed work meant navigating into every lesson.

But the deeper structural problem was the curriculum programs themselves. LEAP had grown across different math curricula — CBSE Grade 4, ICSE Grade 5, Common Core US Grade 6, and more. Each was its own locked "program" with its own chapter structure. If a student finished CBSE Grade 4 math and needed advanced practice, the tutor had to assign an entirely separate program — bringing in a whole new set of chapters. After assigning multiple programs, tracking became impossible: which chapters belonged to which program? Which had homework? What was the student's actual progress? Within those chapters, the UI itself was cluttered — inconsistent node types, unclear lesson structures, missing visual language.

The cost was real. Tutors couldn't create custom learning paths ("this student is ready for Grade 5 concepts but needs Grade 4 foundations"). Progress tracking broke down across multiple programs. Students had no homework visibility. And the platform had no room to grow coherently.

02 Role

I owned the interaction design, information architecture, and system design end-to-end. My scope: the UX for the goal creation flow, the internal chapter structure and system design (tracking, time, status progression), the page layouts for the learning homepage and chapter content, and the entire homework section.

I worked directly with the PM on requirements and curriculum alignment, 3 engineers on implementation, and the curriculum team to ensure the architecture would work across CBSE, ICSE, and Common Core math programs. Success metrics were defined upfront: a unified chapter structure across all curricula, homework visibility and completion rates, and the ability for tutors to create custom learning paths.

The illustrator and motion designer owned the visual execution — iconography, animations on chapter CTAs, animations on notes, and animation language across the system. We collaborated closely, brainstorming and discussing different possibilities for iconography and interaction patterns before they brought the system to life visually.

One hard constraint: 4 weeks for complete scope (goal flow + homepage + chapter system). We couldn't break the existing data structure during migration.

03 Process

Why goal creation first?

Goal creation was independent and could ship on the existing system, making it the strategic first priority. The insight from tutor feedback was clear: locked curriculum programs weren't working. Tutors needed power to select chapters across programs and curricula to create customized learning paths.

I designed three goal types — School Success (academic prep), Test Prep (competitive exams), and Enrichment (skill acquisition) — each with a guided flow. Tutors select a goal type, then choose grade and curriculum, then the recommended chapters surface. They can add or remove chapters, set a completion timeline, and share the goal with parents for feedback. For Test Prep, the curriculum team pre-mapped chapters to specific exams; for Enrichment, tutors pick from a skills list. This framework meant tutors didn't have to build from scratch.

We explored different progressions for surfacing this information, but this flow (goal type → grade/curriculum → chapters → timeline) felt most sensible based on patterns we observed with tutors. We considered allowing tutors to upload school curriculums and auto-surface chapters, but de-scoped it due to tech and timeline constraints.

What made the chapter system hard?

LEAP had 15+ different node and sheet types — Learn, Practice, Smart Practice, Video, Challenge Arena, Test, Puzzle, Recap, Custom Test, Project, Extra Practice, Download, Activity, Subjective, and more. Each subject had categorized them differently in the old system. We had to create one unified three-level hierarchy (Chapter → Block → Node) without breaking the existing system during migration.

I worked closely with engineers to understand the data constraints. We couldn't deprecate existing structures; we had to accommodate them. Two block types emerged: Lessons (mandatory, count toward progress) and Resources (optional enrichment, don't count). Every sheet type fit into one of these categories. Nodes within each block had consistent properties — states (locked, unlocked, in-progress, completed), conditions (mandatory/optional), locations (in-class/anywhere). This unified structure let us handle new concepts like Competition Arena and Puzzles as resource blocks without fragmenting the system.

Learning Homepage

Homepage priorities — what came first?

I surfaced homework at the top — it's critical for learning progress and ensures concept absorption. It was buried before; now it's the first thing students see. Below homework, I added recent chapters as quick navigation for students with multiple goals; they can jump back to ongoing content without scrolling or recalling which goal they were on. Chapter Structure Hierarchy

Four decisions that shaped the system.

Insight

"The unified architecture let us handle new concepts like Competition Arena and Puzzles as resource blocks — without fragmenting the system."

From: Migration design review, before engineering build.

Decision 01

Three-level hierarchy: Chapter → Block → Node

15+ node types across subjects, each categorized differently. We couldn't break existing data during migration. The solution: a unified three-level structure with two block types — Lessons (mandatory, count toward progress) and Resources (optional enrichment). Every sheet type fit into one category.

What I considered

  • Flat list of node types — rejected: didn't scale, hard for tutors to compose.
  • Per-subject taxonomies — rejected: re-introduced the fragmentation problem we were solving.
  • Chapter → Block → Node (picked) — absorbs existing structures, supports new concepts without rework.

Decision 02

Progress tied to admin location, not teacher location

Teachers need flexibility to move nodes between in-class and anywhere. But the curriculum system needs a reliable signal of what was actually taught. So progress always counts from admin location. Teachers can move nodes temporarily; progress stays tied to the authoritative structure.

What I considered

  • Progress follows teacher location — rejected: tutors could inadvertently break curriculum tracking.
  • Lock node locations — rejected: removed the flexibility tutors needed to adapt.
  • Admin-location authoritative (picked) — both sides get what they need.

Decision 03

Queue cap of 10 homework items

Three homework sources — auto-generated practice, teacher-assigned lessons, system smart practice. Collectively, we landed on 10 as the sweet spot. Overflow queued but hidden; surfaces when load clears. Teachers see the full queue.

What I considered

  • Show everything always — rejected: overwhelmed students, completion dropped.
  • Strict cap at 5 — rejected: under-served students who could handle more.
  • Cap at 10, overflow queued (picked) — visible but bounded; self-paced surfacing.

Decision 04

Teacher review requires student presence

Review only happens when it's useful — when the student is there to discuss. Prevents checkbox-style "marked complete" without the conversation.

What I considered

  • Async review allowed — rejected: teachers would batch-check off completed items without the conversation.
  • No review step — rejected: removed the feedback loop that makes practice meaningful.
  • Synchronous review with student (picked) — preserves the feedback moment.
04 Outcome

Shipped to

28–30k

students across CBSE, ICSE, and Common Core curricula. Goal creation became mandatory — every student's first onboarding step.

Shipped and live in production across the LEAP platform. Tutors adopted all three goal types: School Success for core curriculum, Test Prep for competitive exams, and Enrichment for skill-building.

Before vs. after.

Before. Assigning the right curriculum was a rigid exercise. Tutors picked entire programs (CBSE Grade 4, ICSE Grade 5) or stacked multiple programs for mixed-content students. Tracking what was actually assigned — or how progress added up across programs — was unreliable.

After. True personalization became the default. Every student has a goal. Tutors compose paths from individual chapters across programs, not pre-built bundles. Tracking is centralized around goals. Students see their homework queue on the homepage with clear due-date progression (Day 1–4 normal, Day 5–6 ⚠️, Day 7 overdue).

One de-scoped item: Milestones and achievements (exams passed, medals earned, competitions won). We wanted these on the dashboard but couldn't complete in 4 weeks. Planned for follow-on work.

05 Reflection

Pre-map content during discovery, not during build.

The curriculum pre-mapping for Test Prep should have been done in discovery, not during implementation. It would have surfaced the complexity of exam-chapter relationships sooner and reduced engineering rework. For my next role: any system that depends on content taxonomy gets that taxonomy validated upfront, not concurrently.

Phase the complexity — touch one thing at a time.

The chapter system was hardest because it touched everything. Breaking it into phases (goals first, then homepage, then system) made the complexity manageable. Goal creation could ship independently on the existing system — that bought us learning before we touched the architecture itself.

Constraints force ruthless scoping.

Working backward from a 4-week deadline meant every feature had to earn its place. Goal creation worked because it was independent. The homepage worked because the priorities were clear: homework first, navigation second, planning third. The constraint wasn't a limitation — it was the scoping tool.

What carries forward

For systems that must absorb existing data, design an architecture that expands the existing — don't replace it.
