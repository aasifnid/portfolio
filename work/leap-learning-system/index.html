01 The Problem

The assignment loop that gave nothing back.

Teachers assigned programmes. Students completed work. But between assignment and the next session, there was no signal — no summary, no status, nothing to act on without drilling into each chapter manually.

The platform had content. What it didn't have was a model that made learning trackable. Programmes were content-first — teachers pushed pre-packaged material without defining what it was working toward. Progress was logged at the node level, but never surfaced in an aggregated, readable form. Understanding where a student stood required opening chapters one by one.

Parents had no independent access at all. Progress required a teacher to narrate it. Every update was a manual conversation, not a product feature.

No progress summary

Tracking required drilling chapter by chapter. There was no aggregated view of what a student had completed or where they were heading.

Parents waiting

Parents had no independent view of progress. Every update required a teacher to narrate it — a manual intervention that the product should have handled.

No assignment target

Programmes were content-first. Teachers assigned material without a goal — and had no way to know if the content was moving the student toward one.

No homework surface

Students had no visibility into assigned work outside sessions. Homework existed as a verbal instruction, not a product feature.

02 Role

Sole designer — from model definition to all three surfaces.

Product designer, end-to-end. I translated teacher feedback into a new assignment model, designed the goal creation tool, defined the unified chapter architecture that makes progress trackable at every level, and built the learning homepage for three stakeholders simultaneously. The homework queue and its review lifecycle were part of the same sequence.

03 Discovery

The problem wasn't missing features — it was the wrong model.

Teacher feedback made the gap visible. Teachers weren't asking for new screens. They were describing structural friction: the work of manually checking each student's progress, the difficulty of communicating it to parents, the absence of any target for the content they were assigning.

The programme-based model was designed around content delivery. Teachers pushed pre-packaged material, and the system logged completion at the node level. But there was no aggregating layer — no goal for the content to move toward, no view that surfaced what was done and what was outstanding without opening each chapter individually.

The diagnosis: personalisation was impossible because visibility was impossible. Before we could build a better homework surface, the underlying model had to change.

The fix wasn't additive. It required replacing the assignment model — not extending it.
04 Key Decisions

Five decisions that built the system.

Decision 01

Goal model over programme assignment

Teachers needed a target, not just a content queue. Moving from programmes — pre-packaged content batches — to goals — teacher-defined objectives with assigned content — gave assignments a direction. Not "assign these chapters" but "reach this by here." Everything else follows: the chapter architecture has something to track toward, the homepage has something to surface, and teachers finally have a return signal after assignment.

Decision 02

Three-tier architecture — one structure, every subject

A chapter contains blocks (lesson or resource), and each block contains nodes (individual activities). Mandatory nodes count toward chapter progress; resource blocks never do. This isn't a policy — it's structural. The separation is what makes progress data reliable across subjects and grade levels. One architecture, no exceptions, everywhere.

Decision 03

Progress tied to admin location, not teacher location

Teachers can move nodes between in-class and homework without breaking progress tracking. A CW node moved to homework by the teacher still counts as CW progress. Teacher flexibility stays local to the session. Progress data stays trustworthy for the curriculum team. Both sides get what they need without requiring either to trust the other's discipline.

Decision 04

Queue cap of 10 — with a review lifecycle

Capping visible homework at 10 prevents overload. But the more important decision was the lifecycle: homework assigned → student submits → stays in queue until the teacher marks it reviewed in class. Completion and review are different events. The cap and the lifecycle enforce that distinction — neither the teacher nor the student can skip the review step by moving on.

Decision 05

One surface, three stakeholders

Teachers create goals and manage content. Students and parents see the same view — progress, goals, assigned homework — with role-differentiated controls. No separate parent dashboard to design or maintain. The same model that gives teachers visibility gives parents independence. They no longer have to wait for a teacher to narrate progress; the product does it.

05 The Build

Three systems, built in sequence.

Goal creation came first — the new model. Then chapter architecture — the structure that makes tracking possible. Then the learning homepage — where all of it surfaces to teachers, students, and parents at a glance.

Goal creation

Teachers set a learning objective and assign content toward it. The goal becomes the organising unit — chapters, homework, and progress all attach to it. For the first time, assignment had a direction, not just a content list.

Goal creation flow — teacher sets a target, assigns chapters and content, and tracks progress against the goal. Over-assignment warning shown when the student's homework queue is already at capacity.

Learning homepage

The surface where all three stakeholders see the same progress — with role-differentiated controls. Teachers get the student list and management controls. Students and parents see goals, homework status, chapter completion, and sheets — without needing a teacher to narrate any of it.

Student and parent view — same content layer, no management controls. Goals, homework, and chapter completion visible independently.

07 Reflection

What I'd do differently

No post-launch instrumentation

I built the goal creation tool to fix a visibility problem, but never defined how to measure whether it solved it. Goal adoption rate, homework completion within assigned content, parent engagement with the progress view — none of these were instrumented before launch. The 80% review target existed as a design intent, not a measurement. I'd want those metrics live before full rollout, not discovered missing after it.

Parent visibility is still passive

The parent view shows progress but can't act on it. Parents see what's assigned and what's been completed — but there's no notification when a goal is achieved, no alert when homework is overdue, no direct signal that closes the loop without them opening the app. The visibility layer is there. The engagement layer isn't. I'd build that next.

Queue cap was a principled guess

The cap of 10 was a joint design and PM call — a reasonable ceiling to prevent over-assignment. But it was never validated against real teacher assignment behaviour. Now that the feature is live, I'd run the analysis: how often do teachers hit the cap, how frequently does overflow surface, are students seeing what they need to? The cap may be right. It may also need adjusting. We don't know yet.
