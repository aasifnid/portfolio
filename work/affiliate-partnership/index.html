Product Design · Cuemath · 2023–24

Affiliate Partnership

Community-driven distribution for event-based acquisition — built on transparency and fast payouts.

Cuemath shifted from paid marketing to in-person events as its primary acquisition channel, but filling 200+ events every weekend across the US needed a new lever. I designed a three-surface affiliate system: a landing page where community educators sign up, a dashboard where they track referrals and earnings, and an admin tool for Cuemath to manage the programme.

01 The Problem

Cuemath's US acquisition strategy was collapsing under cost. Performance marketing — Meta, Google ads — was the primary lever, but cost per lead had climbed to ₹1 lakh or more per enrollment. The unit economics didn't work. The company was burning cash.

The hypothesis: organize in-person math events instead of buying leads. MathFests would let Cuemath invite parents and students directly to a live experience, show the product in action, and build trust through real interaction instead of ads.

The hypothesis was right. By 2024, in-person events had become Cuemath's primary acquisition channel in the US. The company ran events every weekend across major US cities and regions. Cost per enrollment through events was substantially lower than performance marketing.

But scaling events created a new bottleneck: How do you fill 200+ events every weekend across the US without hiring a massive sales team or reverting to expensive ads?

Cuemath's insight: every US region has community educators — tutors, education consultants, homeschool leaders — who already have trust and reach with parents. If you could incentivize them to promote your events in their local networks, you'd get better event attendance at lower cost than performance ads.

02 Role

I was the sole designer on this project. I owned the entire product — UX, interaction design, and visual assets — across all three surfaces: the affiliate sign-up landing page, the affiliate dashboard, and the admin dashboard. No dedicated PM or researcher was assigned; I partnered with engineering for implementation and ops for payout structure decisions.

Success criteria were defined upfront: affiliates must sign up and receive their unique referral link within 5 minutes; the dashboard must give them a clear reason to log in repeatedly (visibility into referrals, conversions, and earnings); the payout process must be transparent and trustworthy; and all three surfaces must work as one system.

One hard constraint: ops defined a fixed payout budget per event. This meant I couldn't solve "build trust through big payouts." Instead, I had to solve it through transparency and speed — making it crystal clear how much affiliates could earn, and sometimes paying them before the event ended to signal trustworthiness.

How I got to the design.

Three modes of inquiry. No formal user research; this was 0→1 against a tight timeline.

03 Decisions

Trust at the moment of friction.

New affiliates don't trust you. They're thinking: "Will I actually get paid? How much money are we really talking about? Is this a scam or a real opportunity?" Cuemath's instinct was to answer these after the sign-up form. That's backwards. New people don't enter personal details until they trust the system. You need credibility before the commitment.

Insight

"Transparency is a trust mechanism, not a feature. Showing potential affiliates exactly how much they can earn upfront communicates: we have nothing to hide."

From: Competitive audit reframe, week 2.

Four decisions that shaped the programme.

Decision 01

Lead with earnings transparency, not brand mission

Landing page hero: "Earn up to $2,000/month promoting MathFest events." FAQ upfront with explicit payout structure: "$2–5 per successful registration, $100–200 per direct admission." Volunteers are dual-motivated — impact and income.

What I considered

  • Lead with brand mission ("Help children love math") — rejected: affiliates can't pay rent with mission. Felt evasive about the deal.
  • Earnings disclosed only after signup — rejected: introduces distrust at the worst moment, after personal info is in.
  • Earnings disclosed in hero (picked) — signals confidence; affiliates self-qualify before form.

Decision 02

Trust signals before the form, not after

Trustpilot reviews (4.8+), community size ("1,000+ volunteers"), payout terms, and FAQs all placed before the sign-up form. Affiliates answer "Is this legitimate?" themselves, then proceed.

What I considered

  • Hero → Form → Trust signals in footer — rejected in internal testing: significantly lower conversion.
  • Long scroll with form at bottom only — rejected: high-intent visitors couldn't shortcut to action.
  • Hero → Trust signals → Form (picked) — converted significantly higher in two-version test.

Decision 03

Dashboard as the engagement surface, not the landing page

The landing page's job is conversion. Whether affiliates keep promoting is determined by the dashboard — referral link, conversion tracking, earnings, payout history, campaign materials. The dashboard must give them a reason to log in repeatedly.

What I considered

  • Minimal dashboard, email-driven updates — rejected: emails get ignored; passive UX.
  • Static link + monthly summary — rejected: no tight feedback loop on referral behavior.
  • Live dashboard with all signals (picked) — clear reason to log in: "Check if my referrals are converting."

Decision 04

Flexible payout timing as a trust-builder

Payouts normally happen 10 business days after event end — that's a long lag during which affiliates wonder if they'll be paid. Operationally, Cuemath sometimes paid before events ended for affiliates hitting early milestones. We made that visible in the dashboard (assigned → verified → processed → paid, with dates).

What I considered

  • Hide payout timing — rejected: opacity is what affiliates already fear.
  • Standard 10-day-post-event only — rejected: misses the trust-building opportunity of early payouts.
  • Visible payout status timeline (picked) — affiliates see money move; trust compounds.

What shipped.

All three surfaces launched: the affiliate sign-up landing page (public-facing), the affiliate dashboard (post-login, referral link + stats + earnings + campaign materials), and the admin dashboard (internal tool for Cuemath to manage affiliates and process payouts). The programme went live, and educators began signing up and promoting MathFest events.

04 Impact

Shipped

0 → 1

Full three-surface affiliate system live. Educators sign up, promote MathFest events, and earn commissions — all without growing the sales team.

I don't have instrumented metrics on programme impact — activation rate, affiliate retention, event traffic attribution, cost per acquisition. This is a gap. Ideally, we'd know whether the design decisions actually paid off in practice. Did earnings transparency increase sign-up conversion? Did the dashboard keep affiliates engaged?

What the design tells us: the framework I built *assumes* these behaviors would emerge if the incentives were right. The programme was designed on four specific bets: that earnings transparency would signal legitimacy, that trust signals before the form would increase conversion, that a dashboard showing progress would drive repeat engagement, and that flexible payout timing would build affiliate confidence.

Cuemath had shifted from performance marketing to event-driven acquisition. The affiliate programme was designed to fill 200+ events every weekend across the US without hiring a massive direct sales team or reverting to expensive ads. It was the next lever in the acquisition funnel.

05 Lessons

Instrument activation from day one — sign-ups aren't enough.

We tracked sign-ups, but not whether affiliates actually shared their link. A signed-up affiliate who never shares is a dead conversion. Next time: instrument activation from launch (Did they share? When? How many times?) and build a nudge flow. "It's been 48 hours since you signed up. Try sharing your link with one person."

Connected surfaces need simultaneous design — not sequential.

I designed the affiliate landing page, then the affiliate dashboard, then realized ops needed an admin tool. What an affiliate sees must match what an admin can manage — these should be designed together. Designing them in sequence created inconsistencies we had to fix after launch.

Trust is built by visual flow, not by copy.

The FAQ addressed every payout question in text. But text doesn't build trust as effectively as visual flow. A payout status timeline — "Referral assigned → Verified → Processed → Paid" with actual dates — reduces payout anxiety more than any paragraph of copy. Where the user feels at risk, design needs to show motion.

What carries forward

When users are wary, place trust signals before the commitment — never after. Transparency before personal info beats reassurance afterward.
