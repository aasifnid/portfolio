Product Design · Cuemath · 2024

New Trial Experience — reducing drop-off across the funnel by streamlining lead-to-paid flow

Designed an autonomous trial-to-enrollment journey that reduced Academic Counselor intervention by 70%, increased trial conversion by 50%, and shifted enrollment discussions to the moment of maximum intent.

Cuemath's lead-to-paid funnel had too many steps: lead signup, AC outreach, trial scheduling, post-trial counselor follow-up calls over days before enrollment. Each step was a drop-off point. Parents completed trials but enrollment took days of AC follow-ups, during which intent cooled. I redesigned the entire flow to remove friction points, empower teachers to initiate conversations naturally within the trial itself, and enable direct enrollment at the moment of highest intent—right after the session. WhatsApp became the primary communication channel; teachers introduced the model; payments and plan discussion shifted from AC calls to in-class conversation and post-trial direct enrollment.

My Role Product Designer (UX, interaction design, flow architecture)
The Team 1 PM (project lead), 1 developer, 1 FE engineer, multiple BE engineers
Timeline 8 weeks initial design + iterations through 2024

01 Context

Cuemath operated on thin margins in India's market. Efficiency wasn't optional; it was existential. The trial-to-paid funnel had become unwieldy: qualified leads signed up, Academic Counselors (ACs) scheduled trials, teachers ran sessions, then ACs followed up for days with parents to discuss plans and collect payment. Some parents enrolled; many dropped off in the days between trial and follow-up.

The problem wasn't any single step — it was the accumulation. Lead signup, AC outreach, trial scheduling, post-trial follow-up, enrollment calls. Each touchpoint was a friction point. Parents completed trials at peak interest — they'd just experienced the teaching, seen their child engaged, had questions answered live. Then they waited for an AC callback. Intent cooled. Scheduling availability conflicted. The enrollment conversation, which should have happened naturally in the moment of maximum conviction, became a formal sales call days later.

Internally, the model was economically unsustainable. ACs were stretched across every step of the funnel. Teachers taught; ACs did everything else. If we could automate the low-leverage touchpoints and let teachers handle the high-trust moments, we could reduce costs, reduce steps, and improve intent capture. The hypothesis: distribute the conversion process across the journey instead of concentrating it in one person's follow-up calls.

02 Role

I was the sole product designer on this project — early in my career, learning on the job. I owned the end-to-end user experience: signup flow, communication strategy, trial booking mechanics, in-trial teacher positioning, and the post-trial enrollment interface. The intent was to replace a multi-day AC-driven process with an autonomous journey interrupted only at moments where human trust mattered.

I worked with a lead product manager who owned strategy and business metrics, and engineering (developer, FE engineer, multiple BE engineers) to implement. The PM shared research and conversion data; I translated that into flows and interactions. We tested early hypotheses with teachers — asking if they could naturally introduce pricing and class structures without sounding like they were selling. They couldn't. That feedback reshaped the entire design.

Success criteria were clear upfront: maintain the qualified-lead-to-paid conversion rate while cutting AC intervention by 70%. We also wanted to reduce the time between trial and enrollment from days to minutes, and we wanted parents to enroll directly without escalation.

One constraint shaped everything: this had to work on WhatsApp. Parents in India engage reliably on WhatsApp; email and in-app notifications don't have the same trust or response rate. So WhatsApp became the primary communication channel — not an add-on, but the spine of the entire flow.

03 Process

The initial hypothesis and why it failed

We started with the assumption that teachers could simultaneously deliver a great lesson and initiate a sales conversation. We thought teachers would naturally introduce pricing, class formats, and curriculum depth during the trial — building conviction and making enrollment feel like a logical next step rather than a hard sell. This seemed elegant: remove the salesperson, let the product speak for itself.

Early testing with teachers showed this was wrong. When we prototyped it and asked teachers to trial it, the feedback was immediate: splitting attention between teaching and closing degraded both. Teachers focused on the lesson and forgot to mention pricing. Or they mentioned it awkwardly and felt inauthentic. The lesson quality suffered because they were thinking about conversion metrics. Parents felt the awkwardness too.

The breakthrough: we didn't need to remove teachers from conversion — we needed to change *when* and *how* they participated. Teachers are trusted; ACs are not. But teachers are also educators, not salespeople. So the design became: teachers focus entirely on the lesson and building rapport. The enrollment conversation — pricing, commitment, payment — happens after, when intent is highest and the teacher-student relationship has already been established.

The five key design decisions

WhatsApp as the primary channel. Email gets ignored; in-app notifications are buried. WhatsApp is where Indian parents live. Every critical communication — verification, slot confirmation, teacher introduction, session reminder, post-trial enrollment — moved to WhatsApp. The shift meant we had reliable response rates and engagement at each step.

Automated 5-minute recovery for abandoned slot selection. Parents signed up, verified on WhatsApp, and then didn't book a slot. We captured why: they were overwhelmed, distracted, or had questions. Instead of losing them, a 5-minute automatic follow-up message triggered. This small intervention recovered significant drop-off volume without requiring AC escalation.

Teacher introduction message 24 hours before the trial. Instead of an AC confirmation, parents got a personal WhatsApp message from their teacher — name, credentials, a video or photo building credibility. This shifted the relationship: parents weren't signing up for a generic trial; they were meeting a specific person. Teacher-parent rapport started before the session, not after.

In-trial conversation focused on learning, not selling. We created a simple teacher script (not a hard pitch) covering learning path, curriculum design, and the different class formats (1:1, 1:2, 1:4) with their trade-offs — cost, attention, benefit. Teachers could reference these naturally when asked, "What comes next?" or "How does this work?" The script wasn't about closing; it was about answering questions authentically.

Direct enrollment immediately after trial. The moment the session ended, parents saw an enrollment option on their device — not a form, just "Enroll now," with three plan options, pricing visible, and one-tap payment. No AC call necessary. If they had questions, the teacher was still present. If they wanted to think about it, they got reminders, but the path was always direct to enrollment, not through an AC.

Implementation phases

Sign-up and verification. Parents filled a simple form (child's name, preferred time), then verified via WhatsApp. This was their first touchpoint with Cuemath. We kept it minimal — just enough to schedule a trial and stay in touch.

Slot assignment and confirmation. After verification, parents chose from available slots. If they abandoned the flow, a 5-minute auto-message asked if they needed help. Teacher names and credentials appeared with each slot, building confidence in the teacher assignment. Once they selected, the confirmation was immediate — no AC call needed.

Pre-trial teacher connection. 24 hours before the trial, the assigned teacher sent a personal WhatsApp message introducing themselves. This wasn't an AC reminder; it was a teacher making the parent feel expected and welcome. Some teachers added a quick video or photo.

The trial session. Teachers ran the session as usual but had a simple reference script if parents asked about pricing or class formats. The script was designed to feel conversational, not salesy. Teachers demoed 1:1, explained what 1:2 or 1:4 looked like, and let parents decide what fit their needs and budget.

Post-trial enrollment. Immediately after the session, parents saw an enrollment interface. They chose a plan, saw pricing, and could enroll right there with the teacher present if they had questions. Parents who wanted to think about it got WhatsApp reminders — personalized by the teacher, not generic.

04 Outcome

The project shipped and the results aligned with what we aimed for: a 70% reduction in AC manual intervention, a 50% lift in qualified-lead-to-trial conversion, and a 9.6% increase in trial-to-paid enrollment.

The 70% reduction in AC intervention was the primary goal. ACs moved from managing the entire funnel to handling only edge cases: parents with specific questions, payment failures, or special requests. Routine follow-up, scheduling, reminders, and enrollment conversations were now handled by the system and the teacher.

The 50% lift in QL-to-trial conversion came from removing AC availability as a bottleneck. Parents no longer had to wait for an AC to be free to schedule a trial. They chose a slot, picked a teacher, and confirmed immediately via WhatsApp. Turnaround time went from days (AC has to call back) to minutes (instant slot confirmation).

The 9.6% trial-to-paid lift was the most meaningful. Parents enrolled right after the trial, at peak conviction, instead of waiting for an AC callback. The teacher was present, so questions got answered immediately. Intent didn't cool between session and enrollment. Some parents still wanted time to think, but they could still enroll directly when ready — no AC call required.

Beyond metrics, the experience fundamentally changed for all three stakeholders. Parents felt respected (no hard sell, just a teacher they trusted). Teachers felt empowered (they managed their own relationships with families). ACs, freed from routine follow-ups, could focus on complex cases and scaling operations. The model scaled: as trial volume increased, AC headcount didn't have to scale proportionally.

05 Reflection

What I'd do differently: Validate the teacher-led hypothesis earlier, with more teachers and in more varied contexts. We spent weeks on the initial design assuming teachers could sell while teaching. A quick prototype with 5–6 teachers in week 1 would have surfaced that constraint immediately and given us more time to iterate on the solution.

The WhatsApp messaging strategy needed more craft. We chose the right channel, but initial messages were too procedural. "Your trial is on Friday" doesn't feel like a teacher reaching out; it feels like an automated reminder. The teacher introduction message worked because it was personal. We should have invested more in making every message feel like it came from a real person, not a system. This gap hurt engagement in the early iterations.

We didn't have a strong measurement framework for the post-trial experience. We collected feedback from parents and teachers (good), but didn't have systematic analysis of *why* some parents enrolled immediately and others dropped off. We were collecting data before the measurement framework was in place. Better practice: define what questions you're asking before you start collecting answers.

What this taught me: Hypotheses need validation before you get attached to them. The initial design was elegant — teachers sell while teaching — but elegance isn't a substitute for reality. The solution was better anyway: shift the burden to the right moment, not the right person. Teachers aren't salespeople; they're trust proxies. Using them as trust proxies (pre-trial introduction, present during session for questions) was more authentic and more effective than expecting them to close deals.

This was also my first time owning an end-to-end user flow that directly impacted business metrics. I learned that "reduce AC intervention by 70%" is a constraint, not a starting point. It forced clarity: every step in the flow had to earn its place by either building trust or capturing intent. If a step didn't do one of those two things, it was friction. Constraints are design tools.
