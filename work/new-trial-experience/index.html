Product Design · Cuemath · 2024

New Trial Experience — reducing AC intervention by 70% and improving trial conversion 50% through distributed expertise

Redesigned trial-to-enrollment to distribute conversion work across three roles: system automation (WhatsApp, slot booking), teachers (relationships, answering questions), parents (direct enrollment). Achieved 70% AC reduction (primary goal) and improved trial conversion 50% and paid conversion 9.6%.

Cuemath's trial-to-paid funnel was a bottleneck: every step—scheduling, reminders, enrollment—passed through a limited AC team. The brief was to cut AC work by 70% without losing conversions. The obvious solution: have teachers lead conversions during trials. They had credibility; ACs didn't. Testing with 5–6 teachers revealed this failed—teachers introducing pricing mid-trial felt performative, and their lesson quality dropped because attention split. The real constraint: you can't ask one person to teach and sell simultaneously. The actual design: distribute the conversion work. Let the system handle wait (WhatsApp slot picker, automated reminders). Teachers handle high-trust moments (pre-trial intro message, answering questions during trial, discussing enrollment after). Parents handle the enrollment choice directly, with teacher present if they have questions. Result: 70% AC reduction achieved, AND trial conversion improved 50% and paid conversion 9.6%.

01 Context

Cuemath's lead-to-paid funnel was entirely AC-dependent. Every qualified lead passed through an AC at four sequential decision points: lead qualification (call parent, assess fit), trial scheduling (offer slots, coordinate availability), enrollment (discuss pricing after trial), and onboarding (collect parent's preferred class days post-enrollment). This wasn't parallel work—each step waited for the previous one to complete. A single parent journey required 4–6 AC interactions over 7–10 days. With limited AC headcount and rising lead volume, the funnel became a queue management problem.

The friction was real. Trials got delayed waiting for AC availability. Follow-ups stalled after the trial because ACs were handling the backlog of earlier leads. Parents' intent cooled during the waiting periods. By the time an AC called back post-trial, the peak moment of conviction had passed. The AC team wasn't incompetent—they were stretched. Cuemath operated on thin margins in India's market. Hiring more ACs to handle the volume wasn't economically viable. The model had to change.

The brief was direct: reduce AC intervention by 70% while maintaining the QL2P (Qualified Lead to Paid) conversion rate. If the team could automate or distribute the routine touchpoints—scheduling trials, reminders, enrollment conversations, and preference collection—the limited AC team could focus on complex cases: students with special needs, payment issues, retention at risk. The system would scale without scaling headcount.

02 Role

I was the sole product designer on this project—early in my career at Cuemath, learning on the job. This was my first time owning an end-to-end user flow that directly impacted business metrics. I owned the entire redesign: the signup experience, communication strategy across WhatsApp, trial slot booking mechanics, teacher positioning during and before the trial, and the post-trial enrollment interface. The scope was complete—from a parent's first interaction (signing up as a qualified lead) through payment and class scheduling.

I worked with a lead product manager who owned strategy and business metrics, and engineering (one developer, one FE engineer, multiple BE engineers) to implement. The PM provided two critical data points: 40% of qualified leads didn't complete trial booking within 24 hours (waiting for AC callback), and 35% of trial-attendees didn't enroll within 3 days of the session (AC follow-up wasn't reaching them). I reframed this: the problem wasn't AC incompetence; it was AC availability as a dependency.

Then I tested the hypothesis that teachers could replace ACs in the conversion conversation. Prototyped with 5–6 teachers. The result was clear: teachers introducing pricing mid-trial felt performative. They'd naturally ask, "What do you think?" but then shift into sales mode—and parents noticed. Lesson quality also dropped because teachers' attention split. Within 3 weeks, I'd learned the real constraint: you can't ask one person to teach and sell simultaneously. That feedback reshaped the entire design approach toward distributed work—system handles wait, teacher handles rapport, parent handles enrollment choice.

Success criteria were defined upfront and were non-negotiable: maintain the QL2P conversion rate we already had (don't lose any conversions) while reducing AC intervention by 70%. Additionally, we wanted to collapse the time between trial and enrollment from days to minutes, and enable parents to move through the entire journey with minimal human intervention—only at moments where trust and credibility mattered. One constraint shaped everything: this had to work on WhatsApp. Parents in India engage reliably on WhatsApp; email and in-app notifications don't have the same trust or response rate. WhatsApp wasn't just a communication channel—it became the spine of the entire autonomous flow.

03 Process

The Initial Hypothesis and Why It Failed

The initial hypothesis was straightforward: have teachers lead conversions during trials. Teachers had credibility with parents; parents trusted educators more than ACs. If teachers could guide parents through pricing, class formats, and enrollment during the trial session itself, ACs could be removed from the conversion touchpoint entirely.

Testing with 5–6 teachers revealed the mechanism of failure: Teachers' cognitive load during a live session is already maxed—managing pacing, monitoring student engagement, adapting explanations on the fly. Adding "and now convince the parent to enroll" creates a context switch mid-session. Teachers forget the pitch, or shoehorn it in awkwardly at the end when they remember. Parents sense the artifice—a teacher who was natural 5 minutes ago suddenly sounds scripted. The lesson quality suffers because the teacher's attention isn't fully on the student. The close fails because timing is wrong. Both break simultaneously.

The breakthrough came from reframing the problem entirely. We didn't need to remove teachers from the conversion journey—we needed to change *when* and *how* they participated. The insight: distribute expertise across the journey instead of overloading any single role. Teachers are trusted, but they're educators, not salespeople. Stop asking them to do both.

The Five Key Design Decisions

Decision 1: WhatsApp as the primary communication channel. Email gets ignored; in-app notifications are buried. WhatsApp is where Indian parents live. Every critical communication—verification, slot confirmation, teacher introduction, session reminders, post-trial enrollment—moved to WhatsApp. This wasn't a secondary notification; it became the spine of the entire autonomous flow. The shift meant reliable response rates and engagement at each step.

Decision 2: Automated 5-minute recovery for abandoned slot selection. Parents signed up and verified on WhatsApp, then didn't proceed to book a slot. We captured why through feedback: overwhelmed, distracted, uncertain about timing. Instead of losing them, a 5-minute automated follow-up message triggered: "Do you need help choosing a time?" This small, well-timed intervention recovered significant drop-off volume without requiring AC escalation. It was automation at the right moment—not replacing human judgment, but removing friction from a low-judgment step.

Decision 3: Teacher introduction message 24 hours before the trial. Instead of an AC confirmation, parents got a personal WhatsApp message from their assigned teacher—name, credentials, sometimes a video or photo. This shifted the relationship before the session even started. Parents weren't signing up for a generic trial; they were meeting a specific person. Teacher-parent rapport began 24 hours early, not after the session ended.

Decision 4: In-trial conversation guided by a reference script, not a hard close. We considered three approaches: (1) Hard script—teachers memorize pricing and benefits, recite before close (rejected: feels scripted, adds cognitive load). (2) Soft talking points—teachers keep pricing on a reference card, mention if parents ask (chosen for trial). (3) No teacher mention, rely on post-trial enrollment UI (rejected: parent questions mid-trial would go unanswered). We chose option 2: teachers have talking points but only deploy them if parents ask "What comes next?" This way the teacher isn't pushing; the parent is pulling. Teachers could reference class format trade-offs (1:1, 1:2, 1:4) with their financial and educational benefits naturally.

Decision 5: Direct enrollment immediately after the trial, with teacher present. The moment the session ended, parents saw an enrollment interface on their device. Not a form—just "Enroll now" with three plan options, pricing visible, and one-tap payment. No AC call necessary. If they had questions, the teacher was still present to answer them. If they wanted time to think, they got personalized WhatsApp reminders from the teacher, but the path was always direct to enrollment, not through an AC.

Implementation Phases

Sign-up and verification. Parents filled a simple form capturing child's name and preferred availability. Then they verified via WhatsApp. This was their first touchpoint with Cuemath. We kept it minimal—just enough information to schedule a trial and establish a communication channel.

Slot selection and automatic recovery. After verification, parents chose from available slots. Teacher names and credentials appeared with each slot, building confidence. If parents abandoned the flow, a 5-minute auto-message offered help. Once they selected, confirmation was immediate—no AC call needed.

Pre-trial teacher connection. 24 hours before the trial, the assigned teacher sent a personal WhatsApp introducing themselves. This was teacher-to-parent, not AC-to-parent. It made parents feel expected and welcome. Some teachers added a quick video or photo to build credibility further.

The trial session itself. Teachers ran the session as usual but had a reference script if parents asked about pricing or class formats. The script was conversational, not salesy. Teachers answered questions authentically about what different class sizes offered.

Post-trial enrollment and preference collection. Immediately after the session, parents saw the enrollment interface. They chose a plan, saw pricing, and could enroll with the teacher present. Crucially, during this enrollment flow, parents also specified their preferred class timing and days for actual instruction. This preference collection—which was previously an AC task done days after enrollment—became part of the immediate post-trial experience. Parents answered it while the trial was fresh in their mind and the teacher was available to help.

04 Outcome

The project shipped and the results aligned with—and exceeded—what we aimed for. We achieved the 70% reduction in AC manual intervention. We maintained the QL2P conversion rate. And we improved trial conversion by 50% and trial-to-paid conversion by 9.6%.

The 70% AC Intervention Reduction

We shipped the entire system (WhatsApp + self-serve + teacher intro + direct enrollment) together, so we can't isolate which single lever drove the 50% trial conversion lift. But the data suggests where it came from: 78% of parents who saw the teacher intro message completed slot booking (vs. ~40% in the old AC-callback model). This suggests pre-trial relationship-building was the primary driver, not just removing the waiting step. The remaining 22% drop-off on slot selection happened even with teacher intro, which tells us some friction remains there—perhaps scheduling complexity or timezone confusion. Future work: A/B test reducing complexity in slot selection (e.g., pre-filled time recommendations vs. open picker).

ACs moved from managing every touchpoint of the funnel to handling only edge cases: parents with specific questions, payment failures, special requests, or students flagged for retention risk. Routine tasks—lead qualification calls, scheduling, reminders, enrollment conversations, preference collection—were now handled by the system, teachers, or parents themselves. The limited AC team, finally freed from repetitive work, could focus on high-judgment situations that actually required their expertise and relationship-building skills.

The 50% Lift in QL-to-Trial Conversion

Trial booking time collapsed from 48–72 hours (AC callback latency + scheduling back-and-forth) to <5 minutes (WhatsApp slot picker). Slot selection, teacher matching, and confirmation became self-serve, happening in minutes instead of days. The friction of "I want to try this, but I have to wait for someone to process my request" disappeared. Intent didn't cool during a wait for manual coordination and scheduling.

The 9.6% Lift in Trial-to-Paid Conversion

This was the most meaningful outcome. Parents enrolled right after the trial, at peak conviction, instead of waiting for an AC follow-up call days later. The teacher was present, so questions got answered immediately in the moment. When parents gave their preferred class timing and days during the enrollment flow (rather than waiting for an AC to ask them later), they were still engaged with the trial experience. Intent didn't evaporate in a gap between the trial and the next AC touchpoint. The autonomous flow captured intent at its highest point.

The Operational Impact

The limited AC team scaled without growing headcount. As trial volume increased, the model didn't require proportionally more ACs because the system and teachers handled the volume. Post-launch audit showed ACs averaged 2.3 "exception" conversations per week per person (payment issues, cancellations, special needs), vs. 15–18 routine conversations in the old model. ACs became a resource for exceptions, not routine processing. This had downstream effects: lower operational costs, more sustainable margins, and a team that wasn't burning out from repetitive, low-judgment work.

The Stakeholder Impact

For parents: Trial booking time collapsed from 48–72 hours to <5 minutes. They could book on their own schedule, meet their teacher before the session, and enroll immediately after—all without coordinating with a human scheduler. For teachers: Pre-trial relationship-building via teacher intro message became the norm; 89% of teachers sent intro messages. Post-trial, teachers reported no pressure to "close the sale"—they answered questions if asked, then parents chose enrollment on their own. For ACs: 70% reduction held, with clear exception-handling workflows to prevent burnout.

05 Reflection

What I'd Do Differently

Weeks 1–3: I designed the teacher-led conversion flow. Weeks 4–5: engineering built the prototype. Week 6: we tested with 5–6 teachers. The feedback was so clear that by week 7, I'd pivoted to the distributed-expertise model. So we lost 5 weeks on a dead-end hypothesis. A quick lo-fi prototype (paper mockup + 30-min teacher conversation) in week 1 would have surfaced the constraint in week 1, freeing weeks 2–7 for iteration on the actual solution. Lesson: validate hypotheses with users before building.

For the next project, I'd front-load messaging design. Before writing interaction flows, I'd draft 5–10 parent-facing messages and test them with actual parents: "Does this feel like a teacher or a robot?" This would have surfaced tone early, instead of discovering it in post-launch feedback. It's a one-day research activity that could have prevented weeks of messaging refinement.

We didn't have a strong measurement framework for the post-trial experience before we started collecting data. We gathered feedback from parents and teachers (good instinct), but didn't have systematic analysis of *why* some parents enrolled immediately and others dropped off. Better practice: define what questions you're asking before you start collecting answers.

What This Taught Me

Hypotheses need validation before you get attached to them. The initial design—teachers lead conversions—was elegant. Elegance isn't a substitute for reality. The actual solution was better anyway: distribute expertise across the journey instead of overloading any single role. Teachers aren't salespeople; they're trust proxies. Using them as trust proxies (pre-trial introduction, present during the session, available for immediate post-trial conversation) was more authentic and more effective than expecting them to close deals.

This was my first time owning an end-to-end user flow that directly impacted business metrics and organizational operations. I learned that constraints—"reduce AC intervention by 70%"—are actually design tools, not limitations. That constraint forced clarity: every step in the flow had to earn its place by either building trust or capturing intent. If a step didn't do one of those two things, it was friction and deserved to be cut. Working backward from a hard constraint meant we couldn't afford vagueness. Every decision had to ladder back to "does this reduce AC work or does it improve the user experience?" Usually it had to do both.

The breakthrough often comes from reframing, not executing harder on the original hypothesis. We didn't solve the problem by making teachers better at selling. We solved it by asking a different question: "What if teachers don't sell at all? What if we distribute the work differently?" That shift in framing—from "how do we make teachers sell" to "how do we remove the need for selling at all"—is what unlocked the solution.
