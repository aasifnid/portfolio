New Trial Experience — Removed AC bottlenecks from trial-to-enrollment. 70% less AC work, same conversion rates.

Redesigned trial-to-enrollment workflow to distribute conversion work across three roles: system (WhatsApp communication + automated slot selection), teachers (relationship-building + answering questions), parents (enrollment decision-making). Achieved primary goal: 70% reduction in AC intervention while maintaining conversion rates. Secondary wins: QL-to-trial conversion improved 50%, trial-to-paid conversion improved 9.6%.

Cuemath's trial funnel was entirely AC-dependent, creating a queue and stalling conversions. The redesign distributed the work: system handles automation (WhatsApp, scheduling), teachers handle relationship-building (introductions, answering questions), parents handle enrollment decisions (selection and payment). ACs now intervene only for exceptions—special needs, payment issues, edge cases.

01 Context

Cuemath's lead-to-paid funnel was entirely AC-dependent. Every qualified lead required multiple AC touchpoints across the journey: initial qualification call, trial scheduling coordination, post-trial follow-up, and preference collection. Each touchpoint added 6–12 hours of delay as ACs managed competing leads. A typical parent journey took 2–3 days from signup to enrollment decision. With limited AC headcount and rising lead volume, the funnel became a bottleneck—ACs spent their time coordinating, not converting.

The friction was real. Trials got delayed waiting for AC availability. Follow-ups stalled after the trial because ACs were handling the backlog of earlier leads. Parents' intent cooled during the waiting periods. By the time an AC called back post-trial, the peak moment of conviction had passed. The AC team wasn't incompetent—they were stretched. Cuemath operated on thin margins in India's market. Hiring more ACs to handle the volume wasn't economically viable. The model had to change.

The brief was direct: reduce AC intervention by 70% while maintaining the QL2P (Qualified Lead to Paid) conversion rate. If the team could automate or distribute the routine touchpoints—scheduling trials, reminders, enrollment conversations, and preference collection—the limited AC team could focus on complex cases: students with special needs, payment issues, retention at risk. The system would scale without scaling headcount.

02 Role

I was the sole product designer on this project. This was my first time owning an end-to-end user flow that directly impacted business metrics. I owned the entire redesign: the signup experience, communication strategy across WhatsApp, trial slot booking mechanics, teacher positioning during and before the trial, and the post-trial enrollment interface. The scope was complete—from a parent's first signup through payment and class scheduling.

I worked with a lead product manager who owned strategy and business metrics, and engineering (one developer, one FE engineer, multiple BE engineers) to implement. The PM provided two critical data points: 40% of qualified leads didn't complete trial booking within 24 hours (waiting for AC callback), and 35% of trial-attendees didn't enroll within 3 days of the session (AC follow-up wasn't reaching them). I reframed this: the problem wasn't AC incompetence; it was AC availability as a dependency.

I tested a promising hypothesis early: have teachers lead conversions during trials. Testing with 5–6 teachers across 22 trial classes revealed the mechanism of failure: Teachers' cognitive load during a live session is already maxed—managing pacing, monitoring student engagement, adapting explanations on the fly. Adding "and now convince the parent to enroll" creates a context switch mid-session. Parents sense the artifice—a teacher who was natural 5 minutes ago suddenly sounds scripted. The lesson quality suffers because the teacher's attention isn't fully on the student. The close fails because timing is wrong. That constraint redirected the entire approach toward distributed work: system handles wait, teacher handles rapport-building, parent handles enrollment choice.

Success criteria were defined upfront and were non-negotiable: maintain the QL2P conversion rate we already had (don't lose any conversions) while reducing AC intervention by 70%. Additionally, we wanted to collapse the time between trial and enrollment from days to minutes, and enable parents to move through the entire journey with minimal human intervention—only at moments where trust and credibility mattered. One constraint shaped everything: this had to work on WhatsApp. We observed that most teacher-parent communication already happened on WhatsApp, not through our built-in Cuemath app chat. Teachers and parents were far more comfortable sharing information over WhatsApp—it was where they naturally defaulted. Rather than fight this pattern, we made it the system's spine.

03 Process

The Initial Hypothesis and Why It Failed

The initial hypothesis was straightforward: have teachers lead conversions during trials. Teachers had credibility with parents; parents trusted educators more than ACs. If teachers could guide parents through pricing, class formats, and enrollment during the trial session itself, ACs could be removed from the conversion touchpoint entirely.

Testing with 5–6 teachers revealed the mechanism of failure: Teachers' cognitive load during a live session is already maxed—managing pacing, monitoring student engagement, adapting explanations on the fly. Adding "and now convince the parent to enroll" creates a context switch mid-session. Teachers forget the pitch, or shoehorn it in awkwardly at the end when they remember. Parents sense the artifice—a teacher who was natural 5 minutes ago suddenly sounds scripted. The lesson quality suffers because the teacher's attention isn't fully on the student. The close fails because timing is wrong. Both break simultaneously.

The breakthrough came from reframing the problem entirely. We didn't need to remove teachers from the conversion journey—we needed to change *when* and *how* they participated. The insight: distribute expertise across the journey instead of overloading any single role. Teachers are trusted, but they're educators, not salespeople. Stop asking them to do both.

The Five Key Design Decisions

Decision 1: WhatsApp as the primary communication channel. We observed that most teacher-parent communication already happened on WhatsApp, not through our built-in Cuemath app chat. Teachers and parents were far more comfortable sharing information over WhatsApp—it was where they naturally defaulted. Rather than fight this pattern, we made it the system's spine. Every critical communication—verification, slot confirmation, teacher introduction, session reminders, post-trial enrollment—moved to WhatsApp. This enabled reliable response rates and engagement at each step without requiring AC coordination. After the trial, teachers shared branded WhatsApp messages with parents covering pricing, class formats, and onboarding timelines. This transformed post-trial follow-up from an AC task into a self-serve, teacher-led experience.

Post-trial WhatsApp card explaining pricing, class formats, and enrollment options. Parents get all the information they need to decide enrollment—no AC call required. Teacher can answer follow-up questions on WhatsApp in real-time.

Decision 2: Automated 5-minute recovery for abandoned slot selection. Parents signed up and verified on WhatsApp, then didn't proceed to book a slot. We captured why through feedback: overwhelmed, distracted, uncertain about timing. Instead of losing them, a 5-minute automated follow-up message triggered: "Do you need help choosing a time?" This small, well-timed intervention recovered significant drop-off volume without requiring AC escalation. It was automation at the right moment—not replacing human judgment, but removing friction from a low-judgment step.

Decision 3: Teacher introduction message 24 hours before the trial. Instead of an AC confirmation, parents got a personal WhatsApp message from their assigned teacher—name, credentials, sometimes a video or photo. This shifted the relationship before the session even started. Parents weren't signing up for a generic trial; they were meeting a specific person. Teacher-parent rapport began 24 hours early, not after the session ended.

Decision 4: In-trial conversation guided by a reference script, not a hard close. We considered three approaches: (1) Hard script—teachers memorize pricing and benefits, recite before close (rejected: feels scripted, adds cognitive load). (2) Soft talking points—teachers keep pricing on a reference card, mention if parents ask (chosen for trial). (3) No teacher mention, rely on post-trial enrollment UI (rejected: parent questions mid-trial would go unanswered). We chose option 2: teachers have talking points but only deploy them if parents ask "What comes next?" This way the teacher isn't pushing; the parent is pulling. Teachers could reference class format trade-offs (1:1, 1:2, 1:4) with their financial and educational benefits naturally.

Decision 5: Direct enrollment immediately after the trial, with teacher present. The moment the session ended, parents saw an enrollment interface on their device. Not a form—just "Enroll now" with three plan options, pricing visible, and one-tap payment. No AC call necessary. If they had questions, the teacher was still present to answer them. If they wanted time to think, they got personalized WhatsApp reminders from the teacher, but the path was always direct to enrollment, not through an AC.

Decisions 1–3 in action: Parents verify via WhatsApp and self-select slots. Teacher names are visible (Decision 3—pre-trial relationship begins here), confirming there's a real person assigned. No AC callback needed. If parents abandon this screen, a 5-minute auto-reminder triggers (Decision 2).

Implementation Phases

Sign-up and verification. Parents filled a simple form capturing child's name and preferred availability. Then they verified via WhatsApp. This was their first touchpoint with Cuemath. We kept it minimal—just enough information to schedule a trial and establish a communication channel.

Slot selection and automatic recovery. After verification, parents chose from available slots. Teacher names and credentials appeared with each slot, building confidence. If parents abandoned the flow, a 5-minute auto-message offered help. Once they selected, confirmation was immediate—no AC call needed.

Pre-trial teacher connection. 24 hours before the trial, the assigned teacher sent a personal WhatsApp introducing themselves. This was teacher-to-parent, not AC-to-parent. It made parents feel expected and welcome. Some teachers added a quick video or photo to build credibility further.

The trial session itself. Teachers ran the session as usual but had a reference script if parents asked about pricing or class formats. The script was conversational, not salesy. Teachers answered questions authentically about what different class sizes offered.

Post-trial enrollment and preference collection. Immediately after the session, parents saw the enrollment interface. They chose a plan, saw pricing, and could enroll with the teacher present. Crucially, during this enrollment flow, parents also specified their preferred class timing and days for actual instruction. This preference collection—which was previously an AC task done days after enrollment—became part of the immediate post-trial experience. Parents answered it while the trial was fresh in their mind and the teacher was available to help.

Teacher side: Structured reference script guides conversation without hard-selling. "Repeat the goal," "Let parent talk," then "Validate & explain." Teachers answer questions authentically about class formats and pricing.

Parent side: Clean, conversational experience. Parents never see the script or sales messaging. Teacher answers feel authentic because they're contextual, not memorized.

Decision 4 proved: The teacher's reference script (left) enables natural parent responses (right). Teachers have talking points but deploy them only when parents ask—parent pulls, teacher doesn't push. No artifice, no performance.

04 Outcome

The project shipped and the results aligned with—and exceeded—our targets.

The 70% Reduction in AC Intervention

ACs transitioned from owning every funnel touchpoint to handling edge cases only. We removed them from routine work—lead qualification calls, scheduling, reminders, enrollment conversations, preference collection. The system, teachers, and parents handled these instead. With routine work automated, the limited AC team focused on high-judgment situations: payment failures, special needs, retention risks. This freed capacity without hiring.

The 50% Lift in QL-to-Trial Conversion

Trial booking time collapsed from 48–72 hours (AC callback latency + scheduling back-and-forth) to <5 minutes (WhatsApp slot picker). Slot selection, teacher matching, and confirmation became self-serve, happening in minutes instead of days. The data bore this out: 78% of parents who saw the teacher intro message completed slot booking within 24 hours (vs. ~40% in the old AC-callback model). Pre-trial relationship-building—a single personal message from the assigned teacher—shifted parents from dependent on AC availability to autonomous. The friction of "I want to try this, but I have to wait for someone to process my request" disappeared entirely.

The 9.6% Lift in Trial-to-Paid Conversion

This was the most meaningful outcome. Parents enrolled right after the trial, at peak conviction, instead of waiting for an AC follow-up call days later. The teacher was present, so questions got answered immediately in the moment. When parents gave their preferred class timing and days during the enrollment flow (rather than waiting for an AC to ask them later), they were still engaged with the trial experience. Intent didn't evaporate in a gap between the trial and the next AC touchpoint. The autonomous flow captured intent at its highest point.

The Operational Impact

The limited AC team scaled without growing headcount. As trial volume increased, the model didn't require proportionally more ACs because the system and teachers handled the volume. Post-launch audit showed ACs averaged 2.3 "exception" conversations per week per person (payment issues, cancellations, special needs), vs. 15–18 routine conversations in the old model. ACs became a resource for exceptions, not routine processing. This had downstream effects: lower operational costs, more sustainable margins, and a team that wasn't burning out from repetitive, low-judgment work.

The Stakeholder Impact

For parents: Trial booking time collapsed from 48–72 hours to <5 minutes. They could book on their own schedule, meet their teacher before the session, and enroll immediately after—all without coordinating with a human scheduler. For teachers: Pre-trial relationship-building via teacher intro message became the norm; 89% of teachers sent intro messages. Post-trial, teachers reported no pressure to "close the sale"—they answered questions if asked, then parents chose enrollment on their own. For ACs: 70% reduction in intervention held, with clear exception-handling workflows to prevent burnout.

05 Reflection

What I'd Do Differently

I spent 5 weeks designing and building the teacher-led hypothesis before testing it. A paper prototype and one 30-minute conversation with a teacher in week 1 would have surfaced the core constraint immediately. Instead, I lost time executing on something that wouldn't work. Lesson: validate assumptions with users before you invest in design.

What This Taught Me

Constraints are design tools, not limitations. The 70% AC reduction mandate forced clarity: every step in the flow had to either build trust or capture intent. If it did neither, it was friction—cut it. That forced me to stop designing features and start designing a system.

The real breakthrough wasn't making teachers better at selling. It was asking a different question: what if teachers don't sell at all? Reframing the problem unlocked everything. Teachers aren't salespeople; they're trust proxies. Using them that way—showing up 24 hours early, being present during the trial, answering questions after—felt authentic and worked better than any script ever could.
