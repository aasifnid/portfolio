01 The Problem

Every demo class ran through a person. That person was the problem.

Academic Counselors sat between every parent and their first Cuemath session. Scheduling, confirmation, reminder calls, rescheduling, follow-up — all manual. On India's thin EdTech margins, that cost didn't scale.

High AC intervention cost

Every trial class required AC involvement at multiple touchpoints — slot selection, reminders, confirmation, post-trial follow-up. Manual at every step.

No self-serve path

Parents couldn't schedule, confirm, or proceed to enrolment independently. AC availability directly gated conversion.

Drop-off between sign-up and trial

Delays in manual slot assignment and follow-up meant parents disengaged before ever attending a session.

Teachers under-utilised for conversion

Teachers had the most credibility with parents but no structured role in the conversion journey — ACs carried that responsibility despite having less product depth.

02 Discovery

The first hypothesis was wrong. That was the finding.

The initial assumption going in: teachers could handle both instruction and conversion simultaneously. If they could demo the product during the trial and close the parent at the end, ACs wouldn't need to be involved at all.

That hypothesis ran into a hard operational limit. Teachers found it nearly impossible to run a quality lesson, manage a student, and actively pitch to a parent at the same time. Splitting attention degraded both — the lesson suffered and the pitch felt unnatural.

The real model that emerged: distribute the conversion across the journey, not concentrate it in one person at one moment. System handles comms. Teachers handle credibility. ACs intervene only where human judgment is genuinely needed.

The question shifted from "can teachers convert?" to "what does each stakeholder do best — and when?"
03 Key Decisions

Five decisions that moved responsibility from people to the system.

Decision 01

WhatsApp as the primary communication channel

Parents in India don't reliably check emails or app notifications. WhatsApp does. Every touchpoint in the comms flow — verification, slot confirmation, teacher introduction, reminders, post-trial follow-up — was built on WhatsApp. Not as a workaround, but as the designed-for channel. Open rates and response rates are fundamentally different on WhatsApp versus any alternative.

Decision 02

Direct mapping — automatic slot assignment by algorithm

Previously, ACs manually matched parents to teachers. The direct mapping system automated this entirely — matching based on teacher preferences, capacity, and subject fit. Parents selected from available slots within 24 hours or on weekends. The match happened in the background. No AC needed for the most manual part of the process.

Decision 03

Teachers own in-trial credibility, not the full pitch

Instead of asking teachers to close the sale, we gave them a structured in-trial script focused on what they do best: demonstrating the product, explaining the 1:2 class format (compared to 1:4 and 1:1), and building personal rapport. The financial comparison and enrolment ask came through the post-trial flow — not from the teacher mid-lesson.

Decision 04

Automated drop-off recovery within 5 minutes

If a parent didn't select a slot within 5 minutes of phone verification, an automated WhatsApp message triggered — not a reminder from an AC, not an email. The 5-minute window was chosen because intent degrades fast at the sign-up stage. Catching parents before they closed the tab mattered more than sending a polished message an hour later.

Decision 05

Immediate post-trial enrolment path

Parents who attended a trial session could enrol directly from the post-trial screen — plan selection and payment without going back to an AC. For parents who weren't ready, the path to rescheduling or requesting another demo was equally direct. Conversion and consideration both had clear, unblocked routes forward.

04 The Build

Four surfaces, one continuous journey.

Sign-up and slot selection

A streamlined registration form captured the child's name, class preference, and preferred timing. WhatsApp-based phone verification replaced email confirmation — matching parents where they already were. Slot selection surfaced options within 24 hours or on weekends, keeping the window tight enough to maintain momentum.

Sign-up flow — WhatsApp verification, slot selection from available windows. Drop-off recovery triggered automatically at 5 minutes.

Auto-communication flow

WhatsApp

Once a slot was confirmed, a sequenced comms flow ran without AC involvement: slot confirmation, teacher profile introduction, pre-session reminder (T-24h), and post-trial follow-up. Teachers were prompted to send a personal WhatsApp message to parents one day before the session — establishing rapport without adding to AC workload.

Auto-comms sequence — slot confirmation → teacher intro → 24h reminder → post-trial path. All triggered without AC action.

In-trial experience

Teachers received a structured flow for the trial session: demonstrate the customised learning path, walk through the curriculum, and present the class format comparison (1:2 vs 1:4 vs 1:1) using a structured side-by-side. The goal wasn't to make teachers salespeople — it was to give them a clear, repeatable script for the 5 minutes of the session that mattered most for parent confidence.

Post-trial enrolment

Both teachers and parents completed short feedback forms after each trial — informing product iteration and surfacing friction. Parents who were ready to enrol could do so immediately from the post-trial screen. Those who needed time had a clear path to reschedule or request further information — without calling an AC.

Post-trial screen — feedback form, immediate plan selection, and reschedule path. Enrolment unblocked from the product.

05 Outcomes

Hit the primary target. Conversion improved as a by-product.

The 70% AC intervention reduction was the brief. The conversion lift wasn't — it came from making the journey faster and less dependent on AC availability at key moments.

06 Reflection

What I'd do differently.

The first hypothesis cost 3 weeks

The assumption that teachers could handle both teaching and conversion simultaneously wasn't tested early enough. We designed for it before validating it. A faster prototype of the in-trial script with 5–6 teachers would have surfaced the operational constraint in week 1, not week 4.

WhatsApp was right but under-designed

We chose WhatsApp correctly — but the messages themselves were functional, not warm. The teacher intro message and pre-session reminder worked, but they read like system output. With more iteration time, the copy and timing of those messages deserved more craft. They're the first human impression a parent gets of Cuemath.

Post-trial feedback wasn't instrumented

We collected feedback from both teachers and parents after every trial. But the data collection wasn't tied to a defined measurement framework — we knew what was being said, but not how to act on it systematically. I'd build the analysis layer before shipping the collection layer next time.
