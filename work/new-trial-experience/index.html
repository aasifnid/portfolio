Product Design · Cuemath · 2024

New Trial Experience

Distributing conversion work across system, teachers, and parents — so AC headcount stops being the bottleneck.

Cuemath's trial funnel was entirely AC-dependent, creating a queue and stalling conversions. The redesign distributed the work: system handles automation (WhatsApp, scheduling), teachers handle relationship-building, parents handle enrollment decisions. ACs now intervene only for exceptions — payment issues, special needs, edge cases.

01 The Problem

Cuemath's lead-to-paid funnel was entirely AC-dependent. Every qualified lead required multiple AC touchpoints across the journey: initial qualification call, trial scheduling coordination, post-trial follow-up, and preference collection. Each touchpoint added 6–12 hours of delay as ACs managed competing leads. A typical parent journey took 2–3 days from signup to enrollment decision. With limited AC headcount and rising lead volume, the funnel became a bottleneck—ACs spent their time coordinating, not converting.

The friction was real. Trials got delayed waiting for AC availability. Follow-ups stalled after the trial because ACs were handling the backlog of earlier leads. Parents' intent cooled during the waiting periods. By the time an AC called back post-trial, the peak moment of conviction had passed. The AC team wasn't incompetent—they were stretched. Cuemath operated on thin margins in India's market. Hiring more ACs to handle the volume wasn't economically viable. The model had to change.

The brief was direct: reduce AC intervention by 70% while maintaining the QL2P (Qualified Lead to Paid) conversion rate. If the team could automate or distribute the routine touchpoints—scheduling trials, reminders, enrollment conversations, and preference collection—the limited AC team could focus on complex cases: students with special needs, payment issues, retention at risk. The system would scale without scaling headcount.

Old system: Sequential AC-dependent steps with 12–24 hour waits between each. New system: Parallel processes across system automation, teacher relationships, and parent self-service. ACs intervene only for exceptions.

02 Role

I was the sole product designer on this project. This was my first time owning an end-to-end user flow that directly impacted business metrics. I owned the entire redesign: the signup experience, communication strategy across WhatsApp, trial slot booking mechanics, teacher positioning during and before the trial, and the post-trial enrollment interface. The scope was complete—from a parent's first signup through payment and class scheduling.

I worked with a lead product manager who owned strategy and business metrics, and engineering (one developer, one FE engineer, multiple BE engineers) to implement. The PM provided two critical data points: 40% of qualified leads didn't complete trial booking within 24 hours (waiting for AC callback), and 35% of trial-attendees didn't enroll within 3 days of the session (AC follow-up wasn't reaching them). I reframed this: the problem wasn't AC incompetence; it was AC availability as a dependency.

I tested a promising hypothesis early: have teachers lead conversions during trials. Testing with 5–6 teachers across 22 trial classes revealed the mechanism of failure: Teachers' cognitive load during a live session is already maxed—managing pacing, monitoring student engagement, adapting explanations on the fly. Adding "and now convince the parent to enroll" creates a context switch mid-session. Parents sense the artifice—a teacher who was natural 5 minutes ago suddenly sounds scripted. The lesson quality suffers because the teacher's attention isn't fully on the student. The close fails because timing is wrong. That constraint redirected the entire approach toward distributed work: system handles wait, teacher handles rapport-building, parent handles enrollment choice.

Success criteria were defined upfront and were non-negotiable: maintain the QL2P conversion rate we already had while reducing AC intervention by 70%. Additionally, we wanted to collapse the time between trial and enrollment from days to minutes, and enable parents to move through the entire journey with minimal human intervention — only at moments where trust and credibility mattered. One constraint shaped everything: this had to work on WhatsApp. We observed that most teacher-parent communication already happened on WhatsApp, not in our app chat. Rather than fight that pattern, we made it the system's spine.

03 Decisions

The Initial Hypothesis and Why It Failed

The initial hypothesis was straightforward: have teachers lead conversions during trials. Teachers had credibility with parents; parents trusted educators more than ACs. If teachers could guide parents through pricing, class formats, and enrollment during the trial session itself, ACs could be removed from the conversion touchpoint entirely.

Testing with 5–6 teachers revealed the mechanism of failure: Teachers' cognitive load during a live session is already maxed—managing pacing, monitoring student engagement, adapting explanations on the fly. Adding "and now convince the parent to enroll" creates a context switch mid-session. Teachers forget the pitch, or shoehorn it in awkwardly at the end when they remember. Parents sense the artifice—a teacher who was natural 5 minutes ago suddenly sounds scripted. The lesson quality suffers because the teacher's attention isn't fully on the student. The close fails because timing is wrong. Both break simultaneously.

The breakthrough came from reframing the problem entirely. We didn't need to remove teachers from the conversion journey — we needed to change when and how they participated. Teachers are trusted, but they're educators, not salespeople. Stop asking them to do both.

Insight

"The real breakthrough wasn't making teachers better at selling. It was asking a different question: what if teachers don't sell at all?"

From: 22 trial sessions, 5–6 teachers, week 5 of the test.

The five design decisions that distributed the work.

Decision 01

WhatsApp as the primary communication channel

Most teacher-parent communication already happened on WhatsApp, not in our built-in app chat. Rather than fight that pattern, we made it the system's spine. Every critical step — verification, slot confirmation, teacher intro, reminders, post-trial enrollment — moved to WhatsApp.

What I considered

  • Build it inside the Cuemath app — rejected: low engagement, parents had to remember to open it.
  • SMS + email — rejected: low response rates; parents didn't trust the channel for transaction.
  • WhatsApp Business API (picked) — high reliability, already-trusted channel, supports rich collateral.

After the trial, teachers shared branded WhatsApp messages covering pricing, class formats, and onboarding timelines. Post-trial follow-up became a teacher-led, self-serve experience — no AC required.

Pricing breakdown: Clear monthly costs including taxes. No surprises, no hidden fees.

Payment flexibility: Monthly installment options for committed families.

Refund guarantee: Clear exit policy builds confidence in the purchase decision.

Class format option 1: 1:1 solo vs 1:2 pair at different price points.

Class format option 2: 1:2 pair vs 1:4 group — cost vs attention trade-off.

Decision 02

Automated 5-minute recovery for abandoned slot selection

Parents signed up and verified on WhatsApp, then didn't proceed to book a slot. Feedback surfaced why: overwhelmed, distracted, uncertain about timing. A 5-minute automated follow-up triggered: "Do you need help choosing a time?" Small, well-timed, low-judgment.

What I considered

  • AC callback to abandoned parents — rejected: re-introduces the very bottleneck we're removing.
  • Wait 24h, then remind — rejected: intent cools too fast; conversion drops.
  • 5-minute auto-message (picked) — captures intent at peak, no human in the loop.

WhatsApp verification: One-time check that confirms the parent is real and establishes the communication channel.

Slot selection: Teacher names visible, building confidence. Abandoned? 5-minute auto-reminder offers help.

Decision 03

Teacher introduction message 24 hours before the trial

Instead of an AC confirmation, parents got a personal WhatsApp message from their assigned teacher — name, credentials, sometimes a short video. Parents weren't signing up for a generic trial; they were meeting a specific person. Rapport began 24 hours early, not after the session ended.

What I considered

  • AC-sent confirmation — rejected: parent reads it as transactional; no rapport.
  • Generic system reminder — rejected: no personalization, no trust signal.
  • Teacher-sent personal intro (picked) — builds rapport before the session, lifts 78% slot-completion within 24h.

Decision 04

In-trial conversation guided by a reference script, not a hard close

Teachers have talking points but only deploy them if parents ask "What comes next?" The parent pulls; the teacher doesn't push. Teachers can reference class format trade-offs (1:1, 1:2, 1:4) naturally — never as a pitch.

What I considered

  • Hard script — memorize pricing, recite before close. Rejected: feels rehearsed, breaks lesson quality.
  • No teacher mention, post-trial UI only — rejected: in-session parent questions go unanswered.
  • Soft talking points (picked) — teacher answers if asked, doesn't initiate. Authentic, contextual.

Decision 05

Direct enrollment immediately after the trial, with teacher present

The moment the session ended, parents saw an enrollment interface on their device — not a form. Three plan options, pricing visible, one-tap payment. No AC call. If they had questions, the teacher was still present.

What I considered

  • AC follow-up call within 24h — rejected: intent cools; teacher is no longer in the moment.
  • Email enrollment link 1h post-trial — rejected: peak conviction passes; parent moves on to other things.
  • Direct in-screen enrollment, teacher present (picked) — captures intent at peak; +9.6% trial-to-paid.

Implementation Phases

Sign-up and verification. Parents filled a simple form capturing child's name and preferred availability. Then they verified via WhatsApp. This was their first touchpoint with Cuemath. We kept it minimal—just enough information to schedule a trial and establish a communication channel.

Slot selection and automatic recovery. After verification, parents chose from available slots. Teacher names and credentials appeared with each slot, building confidence. If parents abandoned the flow, a 5-minute auto-message offered help. Once they selected, confirmation was immediate—no AC call needed.

Pre-trial teacher connection. 24 hours before the trial, the assigned teacher sent a personal WhatsApp introducing themselves. This was teacher-to-parent, not AC-to-parent. It made parents feel expected and welcome. Some teachers added a quick video or photo to build credibility further.

The trial session itself. Teachers ran the session as usual but had a reference script if parents asked about pricing or class formats. The script was conversational, not salesy. Teachers answered questions authentically about what different class sizes offered.

Post-trial enrollment and preference collection. Immediately after the session, parents saw the enrollment interface. They chose a plan, saw pricing, and could enroll with the teacher present. Crucially, during this enrollment flow, parents also specified their preferred class timing and days for actual instruction. This preference collection—which was previously an AC task done days after enrollment—became part of the immediate post-trial experience. Parents answered it while the trial was fresh in their mind and the teacher was available to help.

Teacher side: Structured reference script guides conversation without hard-selling. "Repeat the goal," "Let parent talk," then "Validate & explain." Teachers answer questions authentically about class formats and pricing.

Parent side: Clean, conversational experience. Parents never see the script or sales messaging. Teacher answers feel authentic because they're contextual, not memorized.

Decision 4 proved: The teacher's reference script (left) enables natural parent responses (right). Teachers have talking points but deploy them only when parents ask—parent pulls, teacher doesn't push. No artifice, no performance.

What changed for each role.

For parents. Trial booking time collapsed from 48–72 hours to under 5 minutes. They could book on their own schedule, meet their teacher before the session, and enroll immediately after — all without coordinating with a human scheduler.

For teachers. Pre-trial relationship-building via the intro message became the norm. Post-trial, teachers reported no pressure to "close the sale" — they answered questions if asked, then parents chose enrollment on their own.

For ACs. The 70% reduction in intervention held, with clear exception-handling workflows. The team scaled without growing headcount as trial volume increased — system and teachers absorbed the volume.

05 Lessons

Validate assumptions before you invest five weeks in design.

I spent 5 weeks designing and building the teacher-led hypothesis before testing it. A paper prototype and one 30-minute conversation with a teacher in week 1 would have surfaced the cognitive-load constraint immediately. I lost time executing on something that wouldn't work. For my next role: any hypothesis touching live-session behavior gets a 1-hour validation conversation in week 1, before any design.

Constraints are design tools, not limitations.

The 70% AC reduction mandate forced clarity: every step in the flow had to either build trust or capture intent. If it did neither, it was friction — cut it. That forced me to stop designing features and start designing a system. I'd take this lens to any optimization brief — translate the constraint into a design question, not a feature list.

The reframe was the work.

The real breakthrough wasn't making teachers better at selling. It was asking a different question: what if teachers don't sell at all? Teachers aren't salespeople; they're trust proxies. Using them that way — showing up 24 hours early, being present during the trial, answering questions after — felt authentic and worked better than any script could.

What carries forward

Distribute expertise across the journey; don't overload any single role. The bottleneck is often a role asked to do too many jobs.
