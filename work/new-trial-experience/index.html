Product Design · Cuemath · 2024

New Trial Experience — reducing AC intervention by 70% while improving trial conversions through distributed expertise

Designed an autonomous trial-to-enrollment journey that achieved the 70% AC reduction target while unexpectedly improving qualified lead-to-trial conversion by 50% and trial-to-paid conversion by 9.6%—by distributing expertise across the journey instead of overloading any single role.

Cuemath had a limited AC (Academic Counselor) team managing the entire lead-to-paid funnel: qualification, scheduling, follow-ups, enrollment conversations. The workload was unsustainable. The brief: reduce AC intervention by 70% and free the team to focus on high-value, complex cases. The initial approach seemed logical—have teachers lead conversions during trials, since parents trusted them more than ACs. Early testing revealed this failed: splitting teachers' attention between teaching and closing degraded both. The breakthrough: distribute expertise across the journey instead of overloading any single role. Teachers focus on teaching and rapport. The system handles low-friction moments (signup, slot selection, reminders). Teachers are present for high-trust moments (pre-trial introduction, during-trial if parents ask questions, immediate post-trial enrollment). WhatsApp became the communication spine; immediate enrollment replaced multi-day AC follow-ups; the flow shifted from AC-dependent to autonomous. Result: 70% AC reduction achieved—and conversions improved by 50% on lead-to-trial and 9.6% on trial-to-paid.

01 Context

Cuemath's lead-to-paid funnel was entirely AC-dependent. Every qualified lead passed through an AC: scheduling the trial slot, sending confirmations, making reminder calls, following up post-trial to discuss plans and collect payment, and then collecting the parent's preferred class timing and days for actual instruction. Cuemath had a limited AC team. They were overburdened. Each AC managed leads, tracked qualification status, made outbound calls, scheduled trials, updated student records, coordinated enrollment conversations, collected scheduling preferences from newly enrolled parents, and managed the handoff to teachers. Too many leads; too few people; too many manual, repetitive tasks that extended well beyond the initial sale.

The result was friction at every step. Parents signed up with intent but had to wait for an AC callback to schedule a trial—sometimes days. During that wait, interest cooled. After the trial, when parents were at peak conviction, they had to wait again for an AC follow-up call to discuss enrollment. Once they enrolled and paid, another AC task awaited: collecting their preferred class timing and days. This wasn't a quick checkbox—it required back-and-forth coordination. The AC team couldn't keep pace. Parents' intent evaporated in the waiting. The limited AC capacity meant some conversations never happened; others were rushed.

The AC bottleneck wasn't a people problem; it was a process problem. Each AC was competent but stretched across too many sequential tasks. Cuemath operated on thin margins in India's market. Hiring more ACs to handle the volume wasn't economically viable. The model had to change. The brief was direct: reduce AC intervention by 70% while maintaining the QL2P (Qualified Lead to Paid) conversion rate. If the team could automate or distribute the routine touchpoints—scheduling trials, reminders, enrollment conversations, and preference collection—the limited AC team could focus on complex cases: students with special needs, payment issues, retention at risk. The system would scale without scaling headcount.

02 Role

I was the sole product designer on this project—early in my career at Cuemath, learning on the job. This was my first time owning an end-to-end user flow that directly impacted business metrics. I owned the entire redesign: the signup experience, communication strategy across WhatsApp, trial slot booking mechanics, teacher positioning during and before the trial, and the post-trial enrollment interface. The scope was complete—from a parent's first interaction (signing up as a qualified lead) through payment and class scheduling.

I worked with a lead product manager who owned strategy and business metrics, and engineering (one developer, one FE engineer, multiple BE engineers) to implement. The PM shared research on conversion drop-off points and AC workload; I translated that into interaction flows, user journeys, and interface designs. We tested early hypotheses with teachers—asking if they could naturally introduce pricing and class formats during the trial without sounding like they were selling. They couldn't. That feedback reshaped the entire design approach within the first few weeks.

Success criteria were defined upfront and were non-negotiable: maintain the QL2P conversion rate we already had (don't lose any conversions) while reducing AC intervention by 70%. Additionally, we wanted to collapse the time between trial and enrollment from days to minutes, and enable parents to move through the entire journey with minimal human intervention—only at moments where trust and credibility mattered.

One constraint shaped everything: this had to work on WhatsApp. Parents in India engage reliably on WhatsApp; email and in-app notifications don't have the same trust or response rate. WhatsApp wasn't just a communication channel—it became the spine of the entire autonomous flow. Every critical touchpoint—verification, slot confirmation, teacher introduction, reminders, post-trial enrollment—had to live on WhatsApp.

03 Process

The Initial Hypothesis and Why It Failed

The initial hypothesis was straightforward: have teachers lead conversions during trials. Teachers had credibility with parents; parents trusted educators more than ACs. If teachers could guide parents through pricing, class formats, and enrollment during the trial session itself, ACs could be removed from the conversion touchpoint entirely. This seemed elegant: use the relationship that already existed (teacher-student) to naturally lead to enrollment. Remove the salesperson; let the product and the educator speak for themselves.

Early testing with teachers revealed this approach was fundamentally flawed. We prototyped the flow and asked 5-6 teachers to trial it. The feedback was immediate and consistent: splitting attention between teaching and closing degraded both. Teachers focused on delivering a good lesson and forgot to introduce pricing. When they remembered to mention it, they felt inauthentic—like they were suddenly switching into a sales role mid-session. Parents sensed the awkwardness too. The lesson quality suffered because teachers were thinking about conversion metrics instead of student learning. The closing didn't happen naturally; it felt forced.

The breakthrough came from reframing the problem entirely. We didn't need to remove teachers from the conversion journey—we needed to change *when* and *how* they participated. The insight: distribute expertise across the journey instead of overloading any single role. Teachers are trusted, but they're educators, not salespeople. Stop asking them to do both.

The Five Key Design Decisions

Decision 1: WhatsApp as the primary communication channel. Email gets ignored; in-app notifications are buried. WhatsApp is where Indian parents live. Every critical communication—verification, slot confirmation, teacher introduction, session reminders, post-trial enrollment—moved to WhatsApp. This wasn't a secondary notification; it became the spine of the entire autonomous flow. The shift meant reliable response rates and engagement at each step.

Decision 2: Automated 5-minute recovery for abandoned slot selection. Parents signed up and verified on WhatsApp, then didn't proceed to book a slot. We captured why through feedback: overwhelmed, distracted, uncertain about timing. Instead of losing them, a 5-minute automated follow-up message triggered: "Do you need help choosing a time?" This small, well-timed intervention recovered significant drop-off volume without requiring AC escalation. It was automation at the right moment—not replacing human judgment, but removing friction from a low-judgment step.

Decision 3: Teacher introduction message 24 hours before the trial. Instead of an AC confirmation, parents got a personal WhatsApp message from their assigned teacher—name, credentials, sometimes a video or photo. This shifted the relationship before the session even started. Parents weren't signing up for a generic trial; they were meeting a specific person. Teacher-parent rapport began 24 hours early, not after the session ended. The teacher became a known entity before the trial, not a stranger showing up for the class.

Decision 4: In-trial conversation guided by a simple script, not a hard close. We created a reference script for teachers covering learning path, curriculum design, and class format trade-offs (1:1, 1:2, 1:4) with their financial and educational benefits. Teachers could reference this naturally when parents asked, "What comes next?" or "How does this work?" The script wasn't about closing; it was about answering questions authentically. Teachers demoed what 1:1 looked like, explained 1:2 and 1:4 options, and let parents decide what fit their needs and budget.

Decision 5: Direct enrollment immediately after the trial, with teacher present. The moment the session ended, parents saw an enrollment interface on their device. Not a form—just "Enroll now" with three plan options, pricing visible, and one-tap payment. No AC call necessary. If they had questions, the teacher was still present to answer them. If they wanted time to think, they got personalized WhatsApp reminders from the teacher, but the path was always direct to enrollment, not through an AC.

Implementation Phases

Sign-up and verification. Parents filled a simple form capturing child's name and preferred availability. Then they verified via WhatsApp. This was their first touchpoint with Cuemath. We kept it minimal—just enough information to schedule a trial and establish a communication channel.

Slot selection and automatic recovery. After verification, parents chose from available slots. Teacher names and credentials appeared with each slot, building confidence. If parents abandoned the flow, a 5-minute auto-message offered help. Once they selected, confirmation was immediate—no AC call needed.

Pre-trial teacher connection. 24 hours before the trial, the assigned teacher sent a personal WhatsApp introducing themselves. This was teacher-to-parent, not AC-to-parent. It made parents feel expected and welcome. Some teachers added a quick video or photo to build credibility further.

The trial session itself. Teachers ran the session as usual but had a reference script if parents asked about pricing or class formats. The script was conversational, not salesy. Teachers answered questions authentically about what different class sizes offered.

Post-trial enrollment and preference collection. Immediately after the session, parents saw the enrollment interface. They chose a plan, saw pricing, and could enroll with the teacher present. Crucially, during this enrollment flow, parents also specified their preferred class timing and days for actual instruction. This preference collection—which was previously an AC task done days after enrollment—became part of the immediate post-trial experience. Parents answered it while the trial was fresh in their mind and the teacher was available to help.

04 Outcome

The project shipped and the results aligned with—and exceeded—what we aimed for. We achieved the 70% reduction in AC manual intervention. We maintained the QL2P conversion rate. And we unexpectedly improved trial conversion by 50% and trial-to-paid conversion by 9.6%.

The 70% AC Intervention Reduction

ACs moved from managing every touchpoint of the funnel to handling only edge cases: parents with specific questions, payment failures, special requests, or students flagged for retention risk. Routine tasks—lead qualification calls, scheduling, reminders, enrollment conversations, preference collection—were now handled by the system, teachers, or parents themselves. The limited AC team, finally freed from repetitive work, could focus on high-judgment situations that actually required their expertise and relationship-building skills.

The 50% Lift in QL-to-Trial Conversion

This came from removing dependencies and waiting time. In the old flow, after signup, parents had to wait for an AC to call, discuss their availability, assign them a teacher, and confirm a slot. Each step required AC involvement. Now, parents signed up and immediately chose their slot via WhatsApp, saw which teacher they'd be assigned to, and got instant confirmation. Slot selection, teacher matching, and confirmation became self-serve, happening in minutes instead of days. The friction of "I want to try this, but I have to wait for someone to process my request" disappeared. Intent didn't cool during a wait for manual coordination and scheduling. The entire booking process collapsed from a back-and-forth with an AC to a self-directed flow that parents completed on their own timeline.

The 9.6% Lift in Trial-to-Paid Conversion

This was the most meaningful outcome. Parents enrolled right after the trial, at peak conviction, instead of waiting for an AC follow-up call days later. The teacher was present, so questions got answered immediately in the moment. When parents gave their preferred class timing and days during the enrollment flow (rather than waiting for an AC to ask them later), they were still engaged with the trial experience. Intent didn't evaporate in a gap between the trial and the next AC touchpoint. Some parents still wanted time to think, but they could enroll directly when ready—no AC call required. The autonomous flow captured intent at its highest point.

The Operational Impact

The limited AC team scaled without growing headcount. As trial volume increased, the model didn't require proportionally more ACs because the system and teachers handled the volume. ACs became a resource for exceptions, not routine processing. This had downstream effects: lower operational costs, more sustainable margins, and a team that wasn't burning out from repetitive, low-judgment work.

The Stakeholder Impact

For parents: The experience felt frictionless. They could book a trial on their own schedule, meet their teacher before the session, and enroll immediately after—all without coordinating with a human scheduler. The teacher was a known entity, not a stranger.

For teachers: They were empowered. Instead of being positioned as salespeople, they were positioned as educators who could answer questions about the learning model. Their relationships with parents started before the trial and deepened during it. They had agency over their own scheduling and could build real connections without the pressure of closing a sale.

For ACs: They were freed. The burden of managing every lead through every step of the funnel lifted. They focused on students and parents who needed actual support—not on processing routine requests they didn't have time for anyway.

05 Reflection

What I'd do differently

Validate the teacher-led hypothesis earlier and more rigorously. We spent weeks designing around the assumption that teachers could lead conversions during trials. A quick prototype with 5-6 teachers in week 1 would have surfaced the constraint immediately—that splitting attention degrades both teaching and closing—and given us more time to iterate on the actual solution. Instead, we discovered the problem through full prototyping, which burned time we could have spent refining the autonomous flow.

The WhatsApp messaging strategy needed more craft from the start. We chose the right channel, but early messages were too procedural. "Your trial is scheduled for Friday at 3 PM" doesn't feel like a teacher reaching out; it feels like an automated reminder. The teacher introduction message worked because it was personal—name, credentials, sometimes a photo. We should have invested that same care into every message from the start. Messaging felt like a system communicating, not a person. This gap hurt engagement in early iterations and was only fixed through continuous refinement.

We didn't have a strong measurement framework for the post-trial experience before we started collecting data. We gathered feedback from parents and teachers (good instinct), but didn't have systematic analysis of *why* some parents enrolled immediately and others dropped off. We were collecting data before the measurement framework was in place. Better practice: define what questions you're asking before you start collecting answers.

What this taught me

Hypotheses need validation before you get attached to them. The initial design—teachers lead conversions—was elegant. Elegance isn't a substitute for reality. The actual solution was better anyway: distribute expertise across the journey instead of overloading any single role. Teachers aren't salespeople; they're trust proxies. Using them as trust proxies (pre-trial introduction, present during the session, available for immediate post-trial conversation) was more authentic and more effective than expecting them to close deals.

This was my first time owning an end-to-end user flow that directly impacted business metrics and organizational operations. I learned that constraints—"reduce AC intervention by 70%"—are actually design tools, not limitations. That constraint forced clarity: every step in the flow had to earn its place by either building trust or capturing intent. If a step didn't do one of those two things, it was friction and deserved to be cut. Working backward from a hard constraint meant we couldn't afford vagueness. Every decision had to ladder back to "does this reduce AC work or does it improve the user experience?" Usually it had to do both.

I also learned that the breakthrough often comes from reframing, not from executing harder on the original hypothesis. We didn't solve the problem by making teachers better at selling. We solved it by asking a different question: "What if teachers don't sell at all? What if we distribute the work differently?" That shift in framing—from "how do we make teachers sell" to "how do we remove the need for selling at all"—is what unlocked the solution.
