01 The Problem

Session quality depended on a guess.

No signal at session start

Tutors had to infer student readiness. If a student was overwhelmed, it became visible only after instruction had already begun — sometimes minutes in.

Instruction time used for regulation

Because the platform didn't account for student state, early session time was spent managing resistance. Teaching began before readiness.

Relied entirely on tutor intuition

No consistent way to assess readiness. Session quality varied by individual judgment, with no shared baseline across tutors.

Feel Check was one of three tools in Cuemath's SEL system — alongside Stickers and Take a Break (Riddle Zone · Calm Zone). This case study covers Feel Check.

03 Discovery

Translating clinical frameworks into a 50-minute math session.

I structured Feel Check around three evidence-based frameworks — Wheel of Awareness, RULER, and Zones of Regulation — mapped to a three-stage sequence: Recognize → Label → Regulate.

The core challenge was product fit. These frameworks come from school counselling and therapeutic settings. Cuemath sessions are 50 minutes, focused on math, often the student's third or fourth activity of the evening. Any feature that felt clinical would be abandoned.

Choice increases engagement. "Do It Your Way" was introduced as a fallback but became one of the most selected activities — 289 picks for G5–8 in the Green zone alone. Students weren't avoiding structure — they were choosing autonomy. This reframed how we thought about the regulation activity menu.

Self-reflection has grade limits. The original scope included G1–8. Through tutor conversations and review with Roots Consulting, it became clear that naming body sensations was too demanding for grades 1–2. This finding reshaped the grade scope entirely.

04 Key Decisions

Four decisions that defined how Feel Check works.

Decision 01

Tutor as observer, not director

Every selection belongs to the student. The tutor sees the same screen in real time but cannot tap or control anything. Keeping it fully private was ruled out — for a student who isn't feeling okay, being left alone with something unfamiliar can add load. So instead of removing the tutor, we removed their control.

Student owns the selection. Tutor owns the exit. Post-launch: 89.3% of students made independent selections even with live AV throughout.

Decision 02

The sequence mirrors how awareness builds

Feel Check runs as: Senses → Body → Heart → Mind. Senses grounds students in their environment before moving inward. Each stage progressively narrows choices — Body informs Heart, Heart informs Mind. The circular Wheel of Awareness became the interface itself, not just a reference. The order is not arbitrary — awareness builds from physical → emotional → cognitive.

Decision 03

Grade vocabulary, not grade UI

The UI is identical across G3–4 and G5–8. What differs is vocabulary and content — the emotional language a 9-year-old uses is not the same as a 13-year-old's. Separate visual systems would have doubled the design and engineering surface. One interface, two content layers: same system, grade-appropriate language.

Decision 04

"Unsure" is a valid answer, not a fallback

1 in 5 students couldn't name their body state — the hardest of the four questions. Unsure was designed as an honest, equally-weighted option. Students who selected it were routed to a gentle grounding activity, not skipped past. Naming uncertainty is regulation too.

05 The Solution

A 3-minute pre-session ritual that starts from readiness.

The complete Feel Check sequence. Student-led throughout. Tutor observes, does not direct.

Check-in: Senses + Body + Heart + Mind. Runs in under a minute.

Regulation: student chooses activity. 1.9 min average, including reattempts.

07 Reflection

What I'd do differently.

No learning impact metric

I built Feel Check to prepare students for learning but never defined how to measure whether that translated into academic outcomes. The SEL research is clear: emotional regulation improves academic performance. What we never designed was a way to prove our product's contribution in our own data. I'd instrument that before full rollout, not discover it missing after.

G1–2 left unaddressed

G1–2 was descoped early — the vocabulary demands weren't developmentally appropriate for 6–7 year olds. The right call for the timeline. But those are the youngest students on the platform, with the least capacity to self-regulate and the least language to name it. I'd build it next — not a simplified version of this tool, but designed from the ground up for that stage.

Sensory quality not fully shipped

Two things didn't ship at the quality I'd have wanted: micro-interactions on the check-in icons and dedicated audio for the regulation videos. When the regulation activity is the experience itself — sensory quality is design, not polish. I'd revisit both for v2.
