# Portfolio Writing Guide

## The Voice

**Authoritative. Specific. First-person. Zero jargon. Conversationally human.**

Write like a designer explaining work to a peer. Use "I noticed," "I was surprised by," "what I didn't expect was."

**Does:**
> "I redesigned the parent progress report after discovering that parents weren't missing the data — they were finding it and not trusting it. The UI showed session time, which parents read as 'learning quality.' It isn't. I changed the primary metric to 'concepts mastered.' Support tickets citing confusion dropped by roughly 40%."

**Doesn't:**
> "We leveraged user research insights to create a more intuitive and user-centric experience that better aligned with our target demographic's mental models."

---

## What Senior Writing Sounds Like

Senior designers write with consequence. Every sentence implies stakes.

**Before:** "I explored different layout options for the dashboard and settled on a card-based design."

**After:** "Three layout directions were on the table. Cards offered the most flexibility for a mixed-content feed, but they risked making the page feel like a notifications list rather than a report. I chose a hybrid — card structure for scannability, but with a persistent summary row at the top that gave parents the one number they actually wanted: is my child making progress?"

---

## Tone Calibration

**Confident, not arrogant.** "I decided X because Y" — not "I single-handedly transformed the product."

**Honest, not self-deprecating.** "We didn't have formal research for this, so I based the direction on support ticket analysis and heuristic review of 3 competitor products" — not "Unfortunately, we weren't able to run proper research."

**Specific, not vague.** "6 parents, recruited from Tier 2 cities, 45-minute sessions" — not "several user interviews with our target demographic."

**Plain, not formal.** Write the way a smart person talks, not the way a corporate document reads.

---

## Writing About 4–5 Years at One Company

Show growth, not timeline. "I worked on onboarding, then the dashboard" is a company directory. Show evolution: early work discovering the domain, mid-career owning complex flows, recent work driving strategy.

Each case study self-contained. Across the set, the reviewer sees a designer who got more sophisticated.

---

## What Kills the Writing

1. **Jargon without substance.** "User-centric approach," "leveraged insights," "seamless experience" — these phrases say nothing. Replace with specifics.

2. **Describing screens instead of decisions.** "The home screen has a navigation bar at the bottom with four icons" → "I moved navigation to the bottom after noticing the top bar was getting missed on longer phones — confirmed by session recordings."

3. **Outcome-free endings.** Every case study must end with what changed. No outcome = no case study.

4. **Over-writing.** A 2,000-word case study that could be 800 words is not thorough — it's unedited. Senior designers edit ruthlessly.
